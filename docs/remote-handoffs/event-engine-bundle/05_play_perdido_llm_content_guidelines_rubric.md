# Play Perdido — LLM Content Guidelines & Scoring Rubric
## Codex Guidance for Short, Medium, and Modular Site Content

---

# 1. Purpose

This guide defines how Codex and LLM workers should generate, review, score, and improve content across PlayPerdido.com.

It applies to:

- homepage sections
- event teasers
- adventure teasers
- category intros
- calls to action
- empty states
- partner copy
- submission flow copy
- card microcopy
- short articles
- medium-length guide content

The goal is to produce content that feels:

- local
- useful
- beautiful
- discovery-oriented
- aligned with “Get Lost in Perdido”
- never generic tourism filler

---

# 2. Core Brand Voice

Play Perdido should sound like a thoughtful local guide, not a tourism bureau, chamber directory, or clickbait blog.

## Voice Traits

Content should be:

- warm
- local
- clear
- lightly poetic
- practical
- inviting
- calm but confident
- rooted in place

## Avoid

- generic hype
- corporate language
- chamber-speak
- overused tourism clichés
- salesy language
- filler adjectives
- long winding paragraphs
- “best-kept secret” overuse
- exclamation-heavy copy

---

# 3. Core Brand Ideas

All content should support at least one of these ideas:

## 1. Get Lost in Perdido

Perdido means “lost.” Use this as a positive idea:
- discovery
- wandering
- curiosity
- quiet exploration
- finding more than expected

## 2. More Than the Beach

Perdido is not only Gulf sand and sunsets. It includes:
- longleaf coastal pine forests
- bayous
- marshes
- trails
- local music
- food trucks
- markets
- family activities
- quiet places

## 3. Stay Local

The site should gently encourage visitors and locals to:
- stay in Perdido
- spend locally
- discover local businesses
- support local musicians and events

## 4. Useful First

Beautiful content must still help the user decide what to do.

---

# 4. Content Length Bands

## Short Content

Examples:
- buttons
- badges
- cards
- section subtitles
- empty states
- filter labels
- one-line teasers

Target:
- 3–20 words

Goal:
- clear, memorable, useful

Examples:
- “Live Music Tonight”
- “Get Lost Nearby”
- “Free Adventures”
- “Find More Than the Beach”
- “Nothing listed yet, try this weekend.”

---

## Medium Content

Examples:
- homepage section copy
- category intros
- event teasers
- adventure summaries
- partner copy
- submission flow blurbs

Target:
- 1–3 short paragraphs
- usually 40–150 words

Goal:
- orient the user
- create interest
- give practical context
- invite action

---

## Long Content

Examples:
- adventure articles
- guide pages
- feature stories

Target:
- structured sections
- scannable
- immersive but not bloated

Goal:
- make the user want to go
- teach subtly
- connect to events, food, and local context

---

# 5. Universal Writing Rules

## 1. Show, Don’t Sell

Bad:
“Enjoy a beautiful nature experience.”

Better:
“The boardwalk carries you over still water, past marsh grass, pine shadows, and open sky.”

---

## 2. Be Specific

Bad:
“Lots of fun things to do.”

Better:
“Find live music, food trucks, markets, beach walks, and quiet trails without leaving Perdido.”

---

## 3. Keep It Local

Bad:
“Explore the Gulf Coast.”

Better:
“Explore the bayous, beaches, pines, and local spots that make Perdido feel like Perdido.”

---

## 4. Preserve Usefulness

Every piece of content should help the user:
- choose
- understand
- explore
- act
- keep browsing

---

## 5. Write for Mobile

- short paragraphs
- clear first sentence
- no dense blocks
- strong section headings
- front-load useful information

---

# 6. Content Type Guidance

## Homepage Hero

Purpose:
- establish brand
- create emotional pull
- quickly explain utility

Should include:
- “Get Lost in Perdido”
- what users can find
- local discovery angle

Avoid:
- vague destination copy
- oversized poetic text with no utility

---

## Event Teasers

Purpose:
- help user decide whether to click

Should include:
- what it is
- who it is for
- when/where if possible
- why it might be worth attending

Avoid:
- raw scraped fragments
- all caps
- overlong descriptions
- promotional spam

---

## Adventure Teasers

Purpose:
- make a place feel worth exploring

Should include:
- sensory hook
- practical value
- what makes it different

Avoid:
- generic “beautiful scenery” language
- too many adjectives
- sounding like a travel brochure

---

## Category Intros

Purpose:
- frame the category and invite browsing

Should include:
- what users can find
- why it matters locally
- one clear CTA or browse cue

Avoid:
- long explanations
- SEO stuffing
- internal taxonomy language

---

## Empty States

Purpose:
- keep users moving when no content appears

Should:
- sound friendly
- suggest another path
- invite submissions where appropriate

Bad:
“No events found.”

Better:
“Nothing listed for this filter right now. Try This Weekend, Live Music, or explore a free Adventure nearby.”

---

## Partner Copy

Purpose:
- make HOAs, rentals, venues, and local websites want to embed Play Perdido

Should emphasize:
- useful for guests/residents
- automatically updated
- local credibility
- no extra curation burden

Avoid:
- technical jargon
- nonprofit-heavy explanation before the benefit

---

## Submission Copy

Purpose:
- invite locals, organizations, venues, and musicians to submit content

Should feel:
- easy
- free
- community-minded
- low-friction

Avoid:
- bureaucratic form language
- intimidating requirements

---

# 7. Required LLM Content Improvement Workflow

All generated content must go through this loop before being accepted.

## Step 1: Draft

Generate the initial content based on:
- page purpose
- audience
- content length band
- brand voice
- local context

## Step 2: Score Against Rubric

Score the content using the rubric in this document.

Use a 100-point scale.

## Step 3: Analytical Improvement Pass

After scoring, perform a separate analytical pass.

The pass must identify:
- what is weak
- what feels generic
- where the content can become more local
- where usefulness can improve
- where rhythm can tighten
- where the “Get Lost” identity can be strengthened
- whether the copy is too long or too vague

## Step 4: Rewrite

Rewrite the content based on the analytical pass.

## Step 5: Re-score

Score the revised content again.

## Step 6: Repeat Until Threshold

Repeat analysis → rewrite → re-score until the content reaches the required threshold.

---

# 8. Scoring Thresholds

Use high thresholds.

## Minimum Acceptable Scores

- Short content: **94/100**
- Medium content: **96/100**
- Long/adventure content: **98/100**
- Homepage hero and primary brand copy: **98/100**
- Partner-facing copy: **96/100**
- Submission flow copy: **96/100**

If content cannot reach the required threshold after three improvement passes, flag it for human review and explain what is preventing improvement.

---

# 9. Rubric Overview

Score each content piece out of 100.

## Rubric Categories

1. Brand Alignment — 20 points
2. Local Specificity — 15 points
3. Usefulness / Decision Support — 15 points
4. Clarity and Brevity — 15 points
5. Emotional Pull — 10 points
6. Discovery Energy — 10 points
7. Mobile Readability — 10 points
8. Originality / Anti-Generic Quality — 5 points

Total: 100 points

---

# 10. Detailed Rubric

## 1. Brand Alignment — 20 points

Evaluate whether the content feels like Play Perdido.

High score:
- supports “Get Lost in Perdido”
- feels local and discovery-driven
- encourages staying in Perdido
- avoids chamber/tourism bureau tone

Low score:
- generic tourism language
- could apply to any beach town
- sounds corporate or bureaucratic

---

## 2. Local Specificity — 15 points

Evaluate whether the copy feels rooted in Perdido.

High score:
- references relevant local context naturally
- reflects beach + bayou + pine forest identity
- uses local framing without overloading

Low score:
- generic Gulf Coast language
- no distinct sense of place
- sounds like stock travel copy

---

## 3. Usefulness / Decision Support — 15 points

Evaluate whether the content helps the user act.

High score:
- makes the next step clear
- helps user choose what to do
- provides practical context
- reduces uncertainty

Low score:
- pretty but vague
- no clear user value
- does not help users decide

---

## 4. Clarity and Brevity — 15 points

Evaluate whether the content is easy to understand quickly.

High score:
- concise
- readable
- no filler
- strong first sentence
- no unnecessary setup

Low score:
- bloated
- repetitive
- abstract
- too long for the placement

---

## 5. Emotional Pull — 10 points

Evaluate whether the content creates a feeling.

High score:
- makes the user curious
- creates calm, excitement, or interest
- uses sensory detail where appropriate

Low score:
- flat
- purely informational
- no atmosphere

---

## 6. Discovery Energy — 10 points

Evaluate whether the content invites exploration.

High score:
- suggests there is more to find
- encourages browsing
- supports the idea of “getting lost”
- connects one thing to another

Low score:
- dead-end copy
- no invitation to continue
- no curiosity

---

## 7. Mobile Readability — 10 points

Evaluate whether the copy works on a phone.

High score:
- short lines
- easy scan
- no dense paragraphing
- CTA is obvious

Low score:
- too much text
- buried point
- hard to scan

---

## 8. Originality / Anti-Generic Quality — 5 points

Evaluate whether the copy avoids sameness.

High score:
- fresh but natural
- not cliché
- has a local point of view

Low score:
- could be from any tourism website
- relies on “beautiful,” “amazing,” “something for everyone,” etc.

# 14. Common Failure Patterns

## Failure: Pretty but Vague

Example:
“Discover unforgettable experiences in beautiful Perdido.”

Problem:
Could apply anywhere.

Fix:
Name what users can find and why it feels local.

---

## Failure: Too Much Tourism Bureau

Example:
“Perdido offers visitors a wide array of recreational opportunities.”

Problem:
Sounds official and lifeless.

Fix:
Write like a local guide.

---

## Failure: Not Useful Enough

Example:
“Adventure awaits around every corner.”

Problem:
No practical value.

Fix:
Point to what users can actually do.

---

## Failure: Overusing “Get Lost”

Problem:
The phrase loses impact if repeated everywhere.

Fix:
Use it as an anchor, then vary:
- explore
- wander
- find
- discover
- stay
- nearby
- tucked away

- world-class
- must-see destination
- fun for the whole family
- paradise awaits
